Turner Syndrome Project

Medical Genetics Research Communication

Goal

Explain Turner syndrome using a patient-centered case story and accurate medical evidence.

Approach

I researched trusted sources, organized findings into clinical sections, and wrote clear explanations.

Outcome

A structured presentation outline with clear definitions, complications, diagnosis, and treatment.

Reflection

This strengthened my interest in medicine by connecting genetics to real monitoring and care decisions.

Key takeaway (why it matters)

I learned how to translate complex genetic information into language that a general audience can understand—without losing accuracy.

Evidence image: assets/Projects%20and%20Research/turner.png

Case Summary: “Meet Sara”
  • Sara is a 10-year-old girl who has always been the shortest in her class.
  • She gets tired easily and has frequent ear infections.
  • Her doctor hears a heart murmur and notices slow growth.
  • A karyotype test shows 45,X, confirming Turner syndrome.
Clinical Features & Complications
  • Short stature and slow growth
  • Webbed neck, low-set ears, broad chest, swelling of hands/feet at birth
  • Delayed puberty, no periods, infertility
  • Heart conditions (bicuspid aortic valve, coarctation of the aorta)
  • Kidney differences, thyroid disease, recurrent ear infections and hearing loss
  • Long-term risks: high blood pressure, aortic enlargement, osteoporosis, diabetes, high cholesterol
Cause, Diagnosis, and Treatment (High-Yield)
  • Cause: all or part of one X chromosome is missing (most commonly 45,X; sometimes mosaic forms).
  • Diagnosis: prenatal ultrasound clues + CVS/amniocentesis; after birth confirmed by blood karyotype.
  • Treatment: growth hormone, estrogen/progesterone therapy, and regular heart/kidney/thyroid/hearing monitoring.
  • Support: foundations and support groups provide education, advocacy, and research updates.
Works Cited (Selected)
  • MedlinePlus Genetics (U.S. National Library of Medicine)
  • GARD (NCATS)
  • Cleveland Clinic
  • Genome.gov (NHGRI)
  • Turner Syndrome Foundation
  • Mayo Clinic

Unsafe Patient Handoffs in Hospitals

Patient Safety Evidence-Based Writing ENG 112

Goal

Explain why unsafe handoffs cause preventable harm and how hospitals can reduce that risk.

Approach

I synthesized research, compared solutions, and built a clear framework (problem → causes → interventions).

Outcome

A research-driven argument supporting structured models (I-PASS / SBAR), training, and EHR supports.

Reflection

This showed me how system design and communication directly affect patient outcomes.

Key takeaway (why it matters)

Safety improves when communication is engineered—standardized, practiced, and supported—rather than left to memory or habit.

Evidence image: assets/Projects%20and%20Research/patient.png

Presentation Highlights (Quick Review)
  • Handoff = transfer of responsibility (shift changes, unit transfers, team transitions).
  • Missing information can lead to delays, wrong decisions, and preventable harm.
  • Structured tools like I-PASS reduce errors and harmful events.
  • Main causes: workload pressure, lack of standard format, limited training, fragmented documentation.
  • Solutions: I-PASS/SBAR + training & simulation + EHR templates + safety culture.
References (Selected)
  • Starmer et al. (2014) — New England Journal of Medicine
  • AHRQ (2019) — SBAR technique for communication
  • Manser & Foster (2020) — International Journal for Quality in Health Care
  • Müller et al. (2018) — BMC Medical Education
  • Tam et al. (2018) — PLOS ONE

Conics Project (MAT 172)

Math Modeling Real Measurement Equations

Goal

Identify conic sections in everyday objects and connect measurements to correct equations.

Approach

I observed objects, measured dimensions, and translated those measurements into standard forms.

Outcome

Concrete examples + equation work showing how circles and ellipses appear in real life.

Reflection

I learned that formulas become easier when they represent something measurable and real.

Key takeaway (why it matters)

Modeling is a bridge: you start with a real object, measure it, and end with a mathematical description that matches reality.

Evidence image: assets/Projects%20and%20Research/conic.png

Introduction

Conic sections appear everywhere—bowls, glasses, trays, and tubes. This project helped me connect the shapes I see to the equations I learn in class.

Examples I Found
  • Circle: bowl (top view)
  • Parabola: tray curve
  • Ellipse: tilted glass opening
  • Line: door frame edge
  • Hyperbola: bottle/tube sides
Equations (Make sure your numbers match)

If the radius is 10 cm, then the circle equation would be: x² + y² = 100.
If your final equation is x² + y² = 49, then the radius is 7.

For a competitive portfolio, consistency matters. Pick one set of measurements and keep the equation consistent.
